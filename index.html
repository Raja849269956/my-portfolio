Problem Statement

Preparing for AI/ML and software engineering interviews is disorganized, expensive, and feedback-free. Generic prep books aren't role-specific. Coaching is unaffordable for most students. Solo practice produces no feedback loop — you answer into the void. Every member of my cohort is on the job market, making this problem both personal and universal.

Solution

A no-code AI chatbot that does two things: (1) generates five role-specific interview questions given a job title, company type, and experience level, and (2) evaluates the user's answers in four parts — what worked, what's missing, what to add, and a stronger version to compare against. It won't hand you a script to memorize because memorized answers fail in real interviews.

My Role

Solo designer and builder of the entire system (part of Group 2 for ideation and testing). I wrote and iterated the full system prompt — the primary engineering artifact. I ran all 15 test scenarios, documented every result, and executed a deliberate mid-project pivot: an earlier concept (Menu Match) was abandoned after identifying it crossed into restricted medical advice territory — a conscious risk management decision.

Architecture & Design Decisions
Input Contract

The bot requires three intake fields before generating anything — role, company type, and years of experience. This prevents hallucinated generic output and forces the user to give the model the context it needs.

Output Schema

Every question set follows a fixed 5-question structure: warm-up → 2 technical → behavioral → curveball, each with a one-line hint on what interviewers are actually listening for.

Feedback Schema

Answer evaluation is broken into four parts: what worked · what's missing · what to add · a stronger version. Deliberately avoids scripted answers — memorized responses fail in real interviews.

Constraint Design

The system prompt hard-blocks salary negotiation numbers, resume writing, travel/booking, and medical advice — all routes that would break the product's value proposition or violate platform guidelines.

Key Technical Insight

"In a no-code chatbot, the value lives in the system prompt, not the language model. GPT-4o-mini didn't care whether it was talking about menus or interviews. The prompt decided the behavior. Most of the design work is in the constraints, not the raw generation."

This is directly applicable to production LLM systems: prompt engineering is a first-class engineering discipline, not a workaround. The same principle governs RAG pipelines, agent tool-use constraints, and safety guardrails at scale.

My Reflection

Going into this lab, I assumed the hard part would be the AI: choosing the right model, tuning parameters, dealing with infrastructure. What actually surprised me was that the model barely mattered. GPT-4o-mini was perfectly capable the moment I gave it a clear, structured prompt. The real engineering challenge was constraint design: deciding what the bot should refuse, how to handle ambiguity, and how to make the output format consistent enough to be useful. That shift in thinking, from "what can the model do?" to "what should I allow it to do?", is something I did not expect to get from a no-code tool, but it maps directly onto how I now think about production LLM systems. Abandoning my first idea (Menu Match) mid-project also taught me something: knowing when to pivot is a skill, not a failure. Catching the medical advice risk early and switching cleanly was better engineering judgment than shipping something broken.
